Predicting Domestic Violence
Victimization in NYC

Project Overview

The New York City Police Department (NYPD) responds to roughly 230,000 domestic incidents each year. At this scale, the ongoing protective services that NYPD's 400+ domestic violence prevention officers can offer survivors are a scarce resource [source]. To address this problem, the UChicago Crime Lab partnered with NYPD in 2016 to build a machine learning model to identify which individuals were most at risk for serious domestic violence revictimization. Using data on over ten years of police-reported domestic incidents—including survivor and officer narratives—we trained a model to make these predictions with a high degree of accuracy. You can read more about the project on the Crime Lab website here.

My Role

I joined this project in its early stages as soon as I was hired at the Crime Lab full-time in January 2017. As the sole data scientist on this project, I worked under the supervision of a Data Science Research Director on all technical stages of this project, from record linkage and data cleaning to final model building and evaluation. During the develpment stage of this project, I learned a lot about natural language processing (bag-of-words, embeddings, topic models) and more complex model-building techniques like stacking multiple base models to create a more accurate "meta-model."


Once the model entered the real-world testing phase, I was responsible for receiving monthly pulls of fresh data, putting that data through our record linkage and prediction pipelines, supplying an updated high-risk list to our partners, and tracking which individuals were referred when.
